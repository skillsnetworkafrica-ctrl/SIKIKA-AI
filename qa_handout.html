1 — Technical Accuracy & Reliability
"How accurate is the speech recognition, especially with African accents?"
Iranzi
Web Speech API leverages Google's models trained on millions of hours. Clear English: 92–97% accuracy. African accents (Kenyan, Rwandan, Nigerian): 85–92% — already better than interpreters where 20–30% content is lost. Technical terms are highlighted separately through our term detection engine.
If pressed: "Building a feedback loop — lecturers correct terms post-session, improving future recognition for their speech patterns."
"What happens when the internet goes down mid-lecture?"
Iranzi
WebSocket maintains persistent connection. Brief drops: auto-reconnects in seconds, transcript preserved. Full outage: lecturer records locally, uploads afterward for async transcription. On campus WiFi, sustained outages are rare — and when they happen, human interpreters can't help if they aren't there either.
"How does whiteboard diagram analysis actually work?"
Iranzi
HTML5 canvas captures every stroke as vector data — shapes, connections, text labels. No image recognition. Engine identifies rectangles, circles, arrows, reads labels, generates natural-language descriptions: "A Client node connects to a Server node via a data flow arrow." Sent via WebSocket and read aloud. Deterministic, not probabilistic — reliable and fast.
"Why not just use Google Live Transcribe or Otter.ai?"
Iranzi
1) Those are individual consumer apps (one person, one device). SIKIKA is one-to-many — broadcasts to 200+ students simultaneously. 2) None have diagram analysis. Professor draws on whiteboard → Google captures nothing. 3) Neither offers sign language output. SIKIKA provides 3 modes from one input. Different category entirely.
"What about data privacy?"
Iranzi
Speech recognition runs in the browser — audio not stored on our servers. Transcripts flow through Django Channels WebSocket, deployable on university infrastructure. We store only text, tied to sessions. Universities control retention. GDPR + Kenya Data Protection Act compliant. On-premise deployment available.
2 — Business & Market
"Who's paying? African universities don't have big tech budgets."
Deogracia
Pricing starts at $2,000/year — less than one semester of interpreter services. Multiple revenue paths: disability councils, USAID, DFID, WHO. Kenya's Persons with Disabilities Act requires reasonable accommodation. We help universities comply at a fraction of current costs. Not asking to add budget — offering a way to meet legal obligations they're already failing.
"$2.9M ARR by year three seems ambitious."
Deogracia
80 university subscriptions out of 1,500+ African universities = ~5% penetration. Average contract $36K/year (still 85% cheaper than interpreters). Start Kenya + Rwanda Y1, East Africa Y2, pan-African Y3. Once one university adopts → peer + regulatory pressure drives others. Disability compliance is a rising tide.
"What's your competitive moat? What stops Google from building this?"
Iranzi
Google could build transcription. Can't easily build a purpose-built classroom platform: diagram analysis + sign output + session management + student customization — all designed for African constraints (low bandwidth, varied accents, limited IT). Our moat: domain specificity + early mover advantage + local partnerships = institutional switching costs.
"How do you make money before universities pay?"
Deogracia
Seeking $250K seed for 18 months: product dev, pilots at 3–5 universities, initial hires. Pilots are free in exchange for data + case studies — our primary sales tool. By month 12, convert to paid. Also eligible for Mastercard Foundation, Skoll Foundation, AfDB disability inclusion grants.

3 — Impact & Inclusion
"How do you measure actual impact on student outcomes?"
Daisy
Three metrics: 1) Content access rate — interpreters deliver 70–80%, SIKIKA delivers 95–100%. 2) Visual content access — previously zero for diagrams, now described in real time. 3) Session replay usage — improves retention by 20–30%. Tracked via built-in analytics: transcript completeness, session duration, replay frequency, satisfaction surveys.
"Isn't this just a band-aid? Shouldn't we train more interpreters?"
Daisy
Absolutely, train more interpreters. Kenya needs ~15,000 to reach adequate coverage. At current rates, that's decades away. Students can't wait decades. SIKIKA provides immediate, scalable access today while human capacity is built. Like solar panels — you build them now while also investing in the grid. Complementary, not competing.
"What about students who prefer human interaction over AI?"
Daisy
That preference is valid. SIKIKA is additive — students with interpreters can still use captions as backup. The real question: what about the 99% of disabled students who have NO support at all? They don't have the luxury of preference. SIKIKA serves the gap, not the replacement.
4 — Technical Roadmap
"The sign language avatar — is it real or future?"
Iranzi
Today: text → visual sign representation. Full 3D avatar with KSL is on 12-month roadmap. Infrastructure is built (text pipeline, WebSocket, student interface). Adding animation layer on top. Evaluating partnerships with SignAll + MotionSavvy. The hardest part — real-time text generation and delivery — is done. Avatar is an interface upgrade, not architectural change.
"How does it scale to multiple languages?"
Iranzi
Web Speech API supports 100+ languages including Swahili, Amharic, Yoruba, Zulu. Architecture is language-agnostic — adding a language is a config change, not code. Sign languages (KSL, ASL, BSL) each need their own avatar model — costlier. Starting with KSL, expanding by demand + partnerships.
"What about STEM content — equations, formulas, code?"
Iranzi
Term highlighting handles STEM vocabulary. Whiteboard engine captures equation symbols + spatial relationships → describes as "x² + 2x − 5 = 0". For code (typically on-screen, not spoken): next integration is screen-sharing analysis — 18-month roadmap.
